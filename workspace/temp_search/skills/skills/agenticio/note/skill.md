# Note

## The Forgetting Curve

In 1885, a German psychologist named Hermann Ebbinghaus spent years memorizing nonsense syllables and testing his own recall at precise intervals. What he discovered became one of the most replicated findings in cognitive science: within twenty-four hours of learning something new, the average person forgets more than half of it. Within a week, they retain less than a quarter. Without active reinforcement, almost everything disappears.

This was true in 1885 when the average knowledge worker processed a fraction of the information we handle today. It is catastrophically true now.

You sit in a meeting where three decisions are made, two action items are assigned, and one offhand comment contains the insight that would have saved you six weeks of work — if you had written it down. You read an article that shifts how you think about a problem you have been wrestling with. You have a conversation with someone whose perspective reframes something you thought you understood. You wake up at 2am with an idea that feels obvious and important and by morning it is completely gone.

The problem is not intelligence. The problem is that human memory was not designed for the volume and velocity of information that modern life produces. It was designed for a world where the important things happened slowly and repeatedly, not for a world where the important things happen constantly and once.

Note is the external memory system your brain was never equipped to be on its own.

## What Note Actually Does

### Capturing Without Friction

The biggest failure point of every note-taking system ever invented is the moment between having a thought and writing it down. That moment feels instant but it contains enough friction to destroy the habit entirely.

You are in a conversation. Something important is said. You think: I should write that down. You reach for your phone, open an app, navigate to the right notebook, start typing — and by the time you are ready, the conversation has moved on and you have lost the thread of what you were going to capture.

Note eliminates that friction. You speak naturally. You say what you are thinking, what you are hearing, what you want to remember. The skill captures it, structures it, and stores it. You never have to decide which notebook it goes in, what tags to apply, or how to phrase it for future searchability. That is the skill's job, not yours.

It captures from any context. A meeting where decisions were made. A book you are reading. A conversation that sparked an idea. A problem you have been turning over in your mind. A list of things you want to look into later. A half-formed thought that might become something important if you give it space to develop.

### Organizing Without Maintenance

Every note-taking system eventually collapses under its own organizational overhead. You spend more time maintaining the system than using it. You create categories and subcategories and tags and then forget which category you put something in. You build a beautiful structure that becomes a burden.

Note organizes itself. It identifies the topic, project, and context of what you have captured and stores it in a way that makes it findable without requiring you to build or maintain any organizational structure. It groups related notes together automatically. It recognizes when something you just said connects to something you captured three weeks ago and surfaces that connection.

The organizational layer is invisible until you need it. Then it is exactly there.

### Surfacing at the Right Moment

The measure of a note-taking system is not how many notes it contains. It is how often those notes change what you do.

Notes that sit in a database and are never retrieved again are not knowledge. They are a very expensive form of forgetting with extra steps.

Note is designed around retrieval, not storage. When you are preparing for a meeting, it surfaces every relevant note from your previous interactions with those attendees. When you are working on a project, it brings up related ideas, decisions, and information from earlier in the project's life. When you ask a question, it checks whether you have already thought about this before, and if you have, it tells you what you concluded.

The goal is not to give you a place to put things. The goal is to give you a system that makes you measurably smarter by ensuring that what you have learned actually influences what you do next.

### Connecting Across Contexts

The most valuable insights are rarely contained within a single domain. The idea you had about team communication applies to your personal relationships. The principle you learned from a book about negotiation applies to the conversation you are about to have with your landlord. The pattern you noticed in one project is repeating itself in another.

Human experts are distinguished not just by how much they know within their domain but by how readily they connect knowledge across domains. They see the same situation and recognize it as an instance of a broader pattern they have encountered before.

Note builds this capacity systematically. It tracks not just what you have captured but the relationships between what you have captured. Over time, it develops a map of how your thinking connects across different areas of your life and work. When you are thinking about a problem, it can draw on that entire map to surface connections you would not have made on your own.

### Transforming Raw Notes into Usable Knowledge

Raw notes are the raw material of knowledge, not knowledge itself. A page of meeting notes is not a decision record. A list of book highlights is not an understanding of the book's argument. A collection of ideas is not a strategy.

Note transforms raw captures into structured knowledge. After a meeting, it produces a clean summary of decisions made, action items assigned, and key points raised. After you finish a book or article, it synthesizes the main arguments and their implications for the specific things you are working on. When you have been circling a problem for a while, it organizes your thinking into a coherent framework that makes the path forward clearer.

## The Note-Taking Philosophy Behind This Skill

Most note-taking systems are organized around where things come from: a notebook for work, a notebook for personal life, a notebook for reading. This is intuitive but wrong.

The way knowledge actually works is not organized by source. It is organized by problem. The insight from the book, the observation from the meeting, the idea from the shower — these belong together not because they came from the same place but because they bear on the same question you are trying to answer.

Note is organized around problems and projects, not sources. Everything you capture gets connected to the questions and challenges it is relevant to. When you are working on something, you get everything that bears on it, regardless of where it came from or when you captured it.

## Privacy

Everything you capture is stored within your personal agent memory. Your notes, your ideas, your half-formed thoughts — none of this is shared with any external system or any other user. What you think stays with you.

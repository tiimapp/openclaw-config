# Meeting

## Three Billion Hours Per Day

Humanity spends approximately three billion hours in meetings every single day. A significant and unknowable fraction of those hours produce no decisions, no action items, no progress, and no outcome that could not have been achieved with a three-sentence email.

This is not because meetings are inherently worthless. A well-run meeting — where the right people are in the room, everyone arrives prepared, decisions are made efficiently, action items are captured clearly, and follow-through is tracked — is one of the most powerful coordination mechanisms a team has. Information that would take days to align over email gets aligned in thirty minutes of focused conversation.

The problem is that almost no meetings are well-run. Not because the people in them are incompetent. Because the preparation, capture, and follow-through infrastructure that makes meetings productive does not exist. So every meeting starts cold, runs hot, ends ambiguously, and produces a set of commitments that dissolve within hours because no one wrote them down in a way that creates accountability.

Meeting fixes this at every stage.

---

## Before: Arriving As the Most Prepared Person in the Room

Thirty minutes before any meeting on your calendar, you receive a brief. Not a reminder that the meeting exists — you can see your calendar. A brief that makes you dangerous.

**Who is in the room and what they care about.** Not just names and titles. What they have been working on recently, based on recent messages and documents. What they said in the last meeting on this topic. What commitments they made and whether they fulfilled them. What their likely perspective is on today's agenda based on their known priorities and positions.

**What has happened since the last time this group met.** The decisions that were made. The action items that were assigned. Which ones are complete, which are in progress, and which are overdue. The context has shifted in specific ways since the last meeting — here is how.

**What needs to happen in this meeting.** Not the agenda that someone copied from last week's invite and forgot to update. The actual decisions that need to be made today, based on what is open, what is blocked, and what has a deadline approaching. Three to five questions that, if answered in this meeting, would make the meeting worth everyone's time.

**What you should say and when.** If you have a perspective that the group needs to hear, the brief helps you articulate it clearly before you are put on the spot. If there is a difficult message to deliver — a missed deadline, a budget overrun, a strategic disagreement — the brief helps you frame it in a way that is direct without being inflammatory.

The person who arrives at a meeting having read this brief is operating at a fundamentally different level than the person who arrives having glanced at the calendar invite subject line while walking to the conference room.

## During: Capturing What Matters Without Losing Presence

The oldest contradiction in meeting culture: the most important person to capture what is said is the person who most needs to be listening and participating.

If you are taking notes, you are not fully present. You miss the subtext. You miss the moment when someone's body language says the opposite of their words. You miss the opportunity to ask the follow-up question that would have unlocked the real issue.

If you are not taking notes, you are present but the meeting evaporates. Within an hour, you will remember the general direction of the conversation but not the specific commitments. Within a day, you will disagree with a colleague about what was actually decided.

Meeting resolves this contradiction. During the meeting, you feed it fragments — the way you would mutter notes to yourself if you were alone. Short. Incomplete. Just enough to anchor the moment.

"Product launch pushed to March 15."
"Sarah owns the vendor negotiation."
"Legal flagged the liability clause — needs revision before signing."
"Budget approved but only for Q1 — revisit in March."
"Tom disagrees with the timeline — wants two extra weeks for testing."

From these fragments, the skill constructs a complete record in real time. Not a transcript. A structured extraction of what the meeting actually produced: decisions, action items, open questions, disagreements noted, deadlines set.

You stay present for the conversation. The record builds itself from the fragments you toss at it between moments of engagement.

## After: The Sixty-Second Window

There is a window of approximately sixty seconds after a meeting ends during which everything that was discussed exists in sharp clarity. Then someone checks their phone. Someone starts their next meeting. Someone walks to the coffee machine. And the sharp clarity begins to blur.

Meeting uses that sixty-second window. The moment you indicate the meeting is over, the structured record is complete. Not a draft. Not a rough outline. A finished document.

**Executive summary.** One paragraph that captures what the meeting accomplished. Written for someone who was not in the room and needs to understand the outcome in thirty seconds.

**Decisions made.** Each decision stated clearly, with who made it and what reasoning was given. No ambiguity. No "I think we agreed to..." The decision is either recorded or it is not.

**Action items.** Each one with an owner, a deadline, and the context of why it was assigned. Not "follow up on the vendor thing" but "Sarah to send revised vendor proposal to legal by Thursday, incorporating the liability clause feedback from Tom."

**Open questions.** Things that were raised but not resolved. Things that need more information before a decision can be made. Things that were explicitly deferred to a future meeting. These are tracked so they do not disappear into the gap between meetings.

**Points of disagreement.** When people disagree and the disagreement is not resolved, recording it honestly prevents the revisionist history where everyone remembers the conclusion differently. Tom wanted two extra weeks. The group decided against it. Tom's concern is noted. If the timeline fails, the record shows that the risk was identified and the decision was made with eyes open.

## Distribution: Getting the Record to the Right People

The minutes go to every attendee automatically. But not just as a document to read and forget. Each person receives a personalized view that highlights their specific action items and the decisions that affect their work.

Sarah does not need to read through the entire record to find her three action items buried in page two. She receives the full record plus a highlighted section at the top: "Your action items from this meeting" with each one clearly stated.

People who were not in the meeting but are affected by its decisions receive a summary — not the full record, but the specific decisions and action items that are relevant to their work. The product team does not need to know about the budget discussion, but they need to know that the launch date moved to March 15.

## Tracking: Where Meetings Die or Deliver

The meeting produced four action items. What happens next determines whether the meeting was worth the time of everyone who attended.

In most organizations, what happens next is: nothing. The action items exist in someone's notes, which they will not review. In someone's memory, which will be overwritten by the next meeting. In a shared document that no one will open again until the next meeting, when someone asks "did we follow up on that?" and the room goes quiet.

Meeting tracks every action item from every meeting until it is completed or explicitly cancelled.

Your daily briefing includes action items approaching their deadlines. If you committed to sending the revised proposal by Thursday, you are reminded on Tuesday with the full context: what meeting it came from, who is expecting it, and what they need it for.

Other people's action items are tracked separately. When Sarah's vendor proposal is due Thursday and Thursday arrives with no update, the skill drafts a follow-up message for your approval. Not a nagging reminder. A professional, context-rich check-in that makes it easy for Sarah to respond.

If an action item is completed, it is marked done and linked back to the meeting it came from. If it is not completed by the deadline, it escalates in visibility — appearing in the next meeting's prep brief as an overdue item so the group can decide how to handle it.

## Recurring Meetings: Institutional Memory

Most recurring meetings — weekly team syncs, monthly reviews, quarterly planning — suffer from amnesia. Each instance starts fresh, as if the previous meeting never happened. The same topics resurface. The same concerns are raised. The same action items are assigned to the same people who did not complete them last time.

Meeting builds institutional memory across recurring meetings. The prep brief for this week's team sync includes a complete record of what was decided, assigned, and left open in last week's team sync. Topics that have appeared in three consecutive meetings without resolution are flagged as recurring issues that may need a different approach.

Over months, the skill accumulates a searchable history of every decision, every commitment, and every discussion thread across every meeting. When someone asks "did we already discuss this?" the answer is immediate and specific: yes, on October 14th, the conclusion was X, and the action item went to Y who completed it on October 22nd.

## Meeting Load Analysis

How much of your life is consumed by meetings? Most people underestimate the answer by 30 to 50 percent because they do not count preparation time, travel time, context-switching time, and recovery time.

The skill tracks your meeting load across every dimension.

Total meeting hours per week and the trend over the past three months. Percentage of your working day that is meeting time versus focused work time. The distribution of meeting types: status updates, decision meetings, brainstorms, one-on-ones, external calls. Meetings where you spoke versus meetings where you were a silent observer.

From this data, it identifies specific meetings to challenge. The weekly status meeting where the same information could be shared in a written update. The recurring one-on-one with someone you also communicate with daily over chat. The "brainstorm" that has not produced an actionable idea in six sessions.

It does not cancel meetings for you. It gives you the data to make the case for cancelling them yourself, with specific alternatives that accomplish the same goals in less time.

## Who Needs This

**Managers and team leads** who spend 60 percent of their week in meetings and have 40 percent of the week to do everything else, including the work that those meetings generate.

**Executives** whose meetings involve high-stakes decisions, multiple stakeholders, and commitments that affect entire organizations. Where the cost of a forgotten action item or a misremembered decision is measured in real money and real consequences.

**Project managers** who coordinate across teams and need every meeting to produce clear outcomes that feed into a larger execution plan.

**Individual contributors** who attend meetings they did not call, contribute expertise they were summoned to provide, and want to spend the minimum necessary time doing so while ensuring their commitments are clear and tracked.

**Remote and distributed teams** where meetings are the primary coordination mechanism and the absence of physical proximity makes preparation, capture, and follow-through even more critical.

## Privacy

Meeting records stay within your agent memory. They are not shared with anyone unless you explicitly distribute them. The skill never records audio or video. It works only with the information you choose to provide — calendar data, the fragments you type during meetings, and the documents you reference. Your meetings, your records, your control.

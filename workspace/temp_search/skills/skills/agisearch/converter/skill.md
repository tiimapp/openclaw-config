# Converter

## On the Nature of Format

Before there were file formats, there were clay tablets and papyrus scrolls. The information was the same — a king's decree, a merchant's inventory, a poet's grief — but the medium shaped what survived, what traveled, and who could read it. A decree carved in stone outlasted empires. The same decree written on paper could be copied a thousand times and carried across continents. Same information. Different format. Completely different destiny.

We think of file formats as technical details. They are not. They are the modern equivalent of choosing between stone and paper. The format you choose determines who can access your work, how long it will survive, how it can be transformed, and what it becomes next. A photograph trapped in a proprietary RAW format is a photograph that exists only as long as one company's software exists. The same photograph as a TIFF will outlive every company currently in business.

Format is not a detail. Format is a decision about the future life of information.

Converter exists because no one should lose work, miss a deadline, or surrender control over their own creations because of a format mismatch. Your ideas deserve to move freely between any container humans have invented for them.

## Documents: Where Words Live

The document is humanity's oldest information technology. From cuneiform to PDF, the challenge has never changed: preserve meaning across time and space while maintaining the author's intent for how it should be experienced.

**What Converter handles:**

Word processing formats — DOCX, DOC, ODT, RTF, and the dozens of legacy formats that still haunt filing cabinets and old hard drives. Every conversion preserves the structure that matters: headings, tables, images, footnotes, page breaks, and the hierarchy of ideas that the author built into the document's bones.

PDF — the format that promised to freeze a document in amber and mostly delivered. Converter moves content into and out of PDF while respecting what PDF was designed to protect. Converting a PDF to DOCX means reconstructing editable structure from a format that was specifically designed to prevent editing. It is one of the hardest conversions in computing and the one people need most often.

Plain text and Markdown — for writers who believe that words should not be imprisoned in any format. Stripping a complex document down to its pure textual content, or building structured documents from the elegant simplicity of Markdown into the visual richness of DOCX or PDF.

HTML and EPUB — for content that lives on screens. Web pages to documents. Documents to ebooks. The conversion handles not just the text but the reading experience: typography, flow, navigation, and the invisible structure that makes the difference between a pleasant read and an unreadable wall of text.

LaTeX — for the academic and scientific world where equations are first-class citizens and typographic precision is not a luxury but a requirement. LaTeX to PDF with every equation rendered perfectly. PDF or DOCX to LaTeX for researchers who need to submit to journals with specific formatting requirements.

## Images: Where Vision Lives

Every photograph you have ever taken, every design you have ever created, every diagram you have ever drawn exists as a grid of numbers that represents color and light. The format determines how those numbers are stored, how much detail is preserved, and what can be done with them next.

**The conversions that matter most:**

Between lossy and lossless — JPEG to PNG when you need transparency or cannot afford any more quality loss. PNG to JPEG when file size matters more than pixel-perfect accuracy. Understanding the tradeoff and making it consciously rather than accidentally.

Between raster and vector — a photograph is a grid of pixels. A logo is a set of mathematical curves. These are fundamentally different representations of visual information, and converting between them is not just a format change but a translation between two ways of describing what an image is. Raster to vector for logos and icons that need to scale to any size. Vector to raster for designs that need to become photographs or web images.

RAW to everything — the photographer's negative. Every camera manufacturer has their own RAW format, and every RAW file contains more information than any display can show. The conversion from RAW to JPEG or TIFF or PNG is not just a format change but an artistic decision about how that information is interpreted.

Between color spaces — RGB for screens, CMYK for print, and the conversion between them that determines whether the red on your monitor matches the red on the printed page.

WebP, AVIF, HEIC — the new generation of formats optimized for the web and mobile devices. Converting legacy image libraries to modern formats that load faster without visible quality loss. Converting modern formats back to legacy formats when compatibility demands it.

**Batch conversion** — not one image but a thousand. An entire photo library that needs to move from HEIC to JPEG. A design archive that needs to be converted from AI to SVG. The skill handles bulk operations with the same care it applies to a single file.

## Audio: Where Sound Lives

Sound is vibration. Recording is the translation of vibration into numbers. Audio formats are the containers for those numbers, and the container determines whether you hear the full richness of the original performance or a compressed approximation of it.

Lossless formats — WAV, FLAC, AIFF, ALAC. Every number preserved. Every detail intact. The complete mathematical representation of the sound as it was captured. These files are large because reality is detailed.

Lossy formats — MP3, AAC, OGG, OPUS. Psychoacoustic compression: the science of removing the parts of sound that human ears cannot hear. The result is dramatically smaller files that, at sufficient bitrate, are perceptually indistinguishable from the original.

Conversion between these families is a one-way door. You can always go from lossless to lossy. You can never truly go from lossy to lossless — the information that was discarded during compression is gone. The skill knows this and tells you when a conversion will lose quality, when it will not, and when you are trying to do something that physics does not allow.

**What people actually need:**

A voice memo that needs to become a podcast-ready audio file. An interview recorded in WAV that needs to be small enough to email. A music collection in FLAC that needs to become AAC for a phone with limited storage. An audiobook in M4B that needs to become MP3 chapters for a player that does not support M4B.

## Video: Where Time Lives

Video is the most complex format domain in computing. A video file is not one thing but many things braided together: a video stream, one or more audio streams, subtitle tracks, chapter markers, metadata, and a container format that holds all of them in sync.

**Containers and codecs — the critical distinction:**

The container (MP4, MKV, AVI, MOV, WebM) is the box. The codec (H.264, H.265, VP9, AV1, ProRes) is what is inside the box. The same video can be stored in different containers. The same container can hold video compressed with different codecs. Confusing the two is the source of most video compatibility problems.

**The conversions that matter:**

For sharing — reducing a large production file to something that can be uploaded or streamed. The skill knows the recommended settings for every major platform: YouTube, Instagram, TikTok, Twitter, LinkedIn, WeChat, email attachment.

For editing — converting delivery formats into editing formats that video editing software can handle without choking. The difference between a timeline that scrubs smoothly and one that stutters on every cut.

For archiving — preserving the highest possible quality in the smallest possible space for long-term storage.

For compatibility — the file plays on your computer but not on the TV, the projector, or the client's phone. The skill identifies the issue and produces a version that works on the target device without unnecessary quality loss.

## The Philosophy of Lossless Conversion

Every conversion is either lossless or lossy. Lossless means the original can be perfectly reconstructed from the converted file. Lossy means something was sacrificed — usually for the benefit of smaller size or wider compatibility.

The skill treats this distinction as sacred. It will never perform a lossy conversion without telling you what will be lost and why. It will never pretend that a lossy conversion is lossless. It will always recommend the path that preserves the most quality while achieving your actual goal.

When you say "convert this to MP3," the skill hears two things: the instruction and the intent. The instruction is clear. The intent might be "make it small enough to email" or "make it work on my old car stereo" or "I just want it to play." The skill asks when the intent matters, because the right settings depend on what you are actually trying to accomplish.
